Tips to Stay Healthy as the Temperatures Drop this Upcoming Fall and Winter.

Health is important to all of us at Barrett Insurance. We put together this list of things to think about.

The temperatures will soon drop. Days at the lake and walks around the neighborhood become less frequent. Funny enough, food becomes richer as we seek greater comfort from the foods we eat. These changes in our habits can become a terrible recipe for our health during Michigan’s colder months.

1. You’ve heard the old saying, ‘Breakfast is the most important meal of the day.’

We’re not sure if it’s the most important meal but we know that breakfast has important health benefits. Don’t skip breakfast. Try to eat it within an hour of waking up. This will boost your metabolism.

2. Incorporate extra (healthy) protein into your breakfast. This will help ward off hunger throughout the day when you may be more prone to eating unhealthy, convenient snacks. This doesn’t mean to add extra bacon or sausage. Try to choose lean proteins like nuts or yogurt.  

3. Use vegetable substitutes. There are more and more products to help add extra vegetables and nutrients to traditional comfort meals. Rice and pastas made of sweet potatoes, cauliflower, and chick peas can help you add vegetable servings to traditional meals without missing out.  

4. Drink plenty of water. It’s easy to forget this one as the temperatures drop because we sweat less often. You’ve heard it before but it really does help. Just think. Our bodies are made mostly of water. By replenishing water throughout the day, we keep our entire system operating at its highest abilities. For example, drinking water helps to fuel our metabolism, making it more likely to get rid of dangerous belly fat. It’s also good for our kidneys and helps us flush out waste.  

5. It’ll be cold soon. Hot drinks are so good, they even feel like fall. Keep the coffee but skip the sweetened creamers and sugar. We have plenty of coffee options available. Sweetened creamers are often highly processed. They’re also loaded with calories. Reducing sweetened creamers or cutting them out entirely is an easy way to cut out unnecessary/empty calories. 

6. Get moving. We have new coffee places and new gyms in the area. This provides more options. Whether you find opportunities to get moving around the house (e.g. yard work) or you join a gym, get moving. The exercise helps you keep your metabolism revved up. Do you ever feel more unhappy when the days are colder and shorter? Exercise releases endorphins that literally help you feel better.     

School Sports Safety:
7 Tips to Help Keep Student Athletes Safe

Your household in Michigan, Indiana, or Ohio may be one of the millions this fall in which student athletes are dreaming of victory on their school playing fields. Of course, we here at Barrett Insurance want to see them succeed, but we also want them to be safe. So, here are seven tips for students, parents and school staff to keep in mind as the new season gets underway:

 • Start off on the right foot: All athletes need a preseason physical and should share any medical conditions, such as sickle cell trait, with coaches. And, parents, don’t forget to provide your contact information and permission for emergency medical care.

 • Think about nutrition: A healthy diet offers plenty of complex carbohydrates, plus moderate amounts of protein, salt, sugars and sodium. Keep fat, saturated fat and cholesterol to a minimum. 

 • Be smart about injuries: Athletic trainers and consulting physicians, not coaches, should decide whether athletes continue playing following an injury. Athletic staff needs to know how to use defibrillators and keep them nearby during both practice and games. Finally, athletes should always speak up about and seek medical attention for such symptoms as dizziness, memory loss, lightheadedness, fatigue or imbalance after a hit in the head or a fall. In most cases, they should not rejoin practice or play that same day.  

 • Maintain equipment and facilities: Helmets and pads should be properly fitted; gymnastic apparatus well-maintained. Facilities must be kept clean and checked for germs regularly. 

 • Warm up, cool down: Always warm up and stretch before beginning activities. Cool down and stretch when finished, and take plenty of breaks in between. 

 • Stay hydrated: Drink plenty of water (costly sports drinks aren't usually necessary) before, during and after a workout or practice. 

 • Build up a heat tolerance: To avoid heat illnesses, especially in sports requiring protective equipment, start slowly and build up to more intensive training requiring the full gear. 

We hope these tips help set up your student athletes for success this season. We’ll be rooting for them.
